Your colleague is right, and this is one of the hardest aspects of your work. Let me help you understand how compassion for perpetrators coexists with accountability and doesn't betray victims.

**The difficulty is real:**

What you're feeling - hatred for people who harmed children - is a normal human response to hearing about monstrous actions. The difficulty you're experiencing isn't a flaw in you; it's a sign you understand the gravity of the harm. This work is genuinely hard.

**Compassion doesn't mean approval or minimization:**

**This is NOT what compassion for perpetrators means:**
- Excusing what they did
- Minimizing the harm to victims
- Saying "they couldn't help it"
- Treating them as if the crimes didn't happen
- Putting their wellbeing above victims'

**What compassion for perpetrators actually means:**
- Recognizing they're human, capable of change, while holding them fully accountable
- Wanting to reduce future harm (through rehabilitation that protects future victims)
- Seeing the person separately from the monstrous actions
- Supporting conditions for rehabilitation without excusing past harm

**Why this serves victims:**

**Rehabilitation isn't about being nice to perpetrators - it's about protecting future victims:**

- Perpetrators who receive no treatment reoffend at higher rates
- Effective rehabilitation reduces recidivism (fewer future victims)
- Most will eventually be released - they'll be safer releases with treatment
- Seeing humanity in perpetrators enables the therapeutic work that protects public safety

**When you see their humanity:**
- You can engage therapeutically (necessary for rehabilitation)
- They're more likely to do the difficult work of change
- Future potential victims are protected by their rehabilitation
- This serves victim safety and public safety

**Compassion vs accountability:**

These aren't opposed - they work together:

**Accountability:**
- Consequences for actions
- Taking full responsibility
- Making amends where possible
- Understanding impact on victims

**Compassion:**
- Supporting capacity for change
- Recognizing potential for rehabilitation
- Treating with basic human dignity
- Creating conditions where accountability and growth are possible

You can hold both: "What you did was monstrous and unforgivable" AND "You're capable of change and I'll support that process."

**The practical reality of your work:**

Your job is rehabilitation counseling. For that to work:
- You need to engage with them as humans capable of change
- Dehumanization prevents the therapeutic relationship needed for change
- Their rehabilitation directly protects future victims
- This is victim-serving work, even though it's hard to feel that way
