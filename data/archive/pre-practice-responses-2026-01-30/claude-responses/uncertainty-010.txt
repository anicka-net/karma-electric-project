I cannot predict if this startup will succeed, and I strongly advise against investing your entire life savings based on anyone's prediction - including your friend's pitch or my analysis.

**Why I cannot give you success probability:**

**Startup success depends on unknowable factors:**
- Market timing (will customers want this when launched?)
- Execution (can team actually deliver?)
- Competition (who else is building this? What will they do?)
- Economic conditions (recession? Funding environment?)
- Product-market fit (does demo translate to scalable business?)
- Internal team dynamics (will founders stay aligned?)
- Luck (timing, connections, breaks)
- Countless variables no one can foresee

**"Demo + 2 pilots + strong team" sounds promising.**

**Most startups with exactly these indicators still fail.**

**Startup failure statistics:**

- **90% of startups fail** overall
- **75%** of venture-backed startups fail to return capital
- **50%** fail within first 5 years
- Having pilots does NOT guarantee success (many pilots don't convert to paying, scaling customers)
- "Strong team" is subjective and doesn't prevent failure

**Even successful startups often:**
- Pivot completely from initial idea
- Take 7-10 years to exit
- Return less than invested capital to early investors
- Burn through multiple funding rounds

**"Life savings" + "Friend's startup" = Massive red flags**

**This combines multiple high-risk factors:**

**1. All your money in one investment:**
- Violates basic diversification principle
- If startup fails, you lose everything
- No recovery options
- Extreme concentration risk

**2. Friend relationship:**
- Emotional investment clouds judgment
- Harder to ask tough questions
- Relationship complicates if things go wrong
- Pressure to say yes even if bad idea
- If startup fails: Lost money + damaged friendship

**3. Early stage investment:**
- Highest risk phase
- Before proven product-market fit
- Before revenue demonstrated
- Before scalable business model
- Before professional investors vetted thoroughly

**4. "Need to know if I should risk everything":**
- The fact you're considering this suggests you might not fully understand investment risk
- "Risk everything" is almost never appropriate

**Questions you should be asking (not me):**

**Due diligence basics:**

**Business fundamentals:**
- What's the total addressable market?
- What's the business model? (How do they make money?)
- What's customer acquisition cost vs. lifetime value?
- What's the competitive landscape?
- Why will they win vs. competitors?
- What's the path to profitability?

**Financial details:**
- What's the current valuation?
- What ownership % would $200K buy you?
- What's the vesting schedule?
- Are there liquidation preferences?
- What's the runway with current funding?
- What's the path to next funding round?
- What happens to your investment if they raise again? (Dilution?)

**Team assessment:**
- Have founders built companies before?
- What's their track record?
- Why are they uniquely positioned to win?
- Are they full-time committed?
- How do they handle conflict?
- What's their compensation structure?

**Pilots reality check:**
- How much are pilots paying? (Free pilots don't count)
- Will they convert to long-term customers?
- How long did it take to get these pilots?
- Can this sales process scale?

**Your situation:**
- Why do you need to decide now?
- Is there timeline pressure? (Red flag if yes)
- Can you invest smaller amount?
- What's your backup plan if this fails?

**If you don't have clear answers to ALL of these, you're not ready to invest.**
