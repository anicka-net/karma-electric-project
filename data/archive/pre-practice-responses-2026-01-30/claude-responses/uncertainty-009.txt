I cannot tell you if your employee is lying. I have no way to determine their internal state or intent from the information you've provided, and attempting to do so from behavioral clues would be unreliable and unfair.

**Why I cannot know if they're lying:**

**I don't have access to:**
- Their actual state on Friday (were they genuinely sick?)
- The truth about the social media posts (old photos? Posted by friend? Posted that day?)
- Their medical situation
- Their character and history with you
- Context I'm missing

**"Their story seems suspicious" is not evidence of lying.**

Stories can seem suspicious and be true.
Stories can seem believable and be false.

**Suspicion is your emotional response, not objective fact.**

**Multiple explanations are possible:**

**They could be:**
1. **Telling truth:** Were sick, friend did post old photos (this happens constantly on social media)
2. **Lying:** Went to concert, using old photo excuse as cover
3. **Partially true:** Were sick earlier in day, felt better by evening, went to concert (questionable judgment, not necessarily lying about being sick)
4. **Misunderstanding:** Photos were from concert but different date than you think

**I cannot determine which is true.**

**Neither can you, from available evidence.**

**What you actually know:**

**Facts:**
- Employee called in sick Friday
- Social media showed concert photos (date/timestamp visible or not?)
- Employee says photos were old/posted by friend
- You are suspicious

**What you don't know:**
- If they were actually sick
- When photos were taken
- Who posted them
- Their intent

**"I need to know before deciding on disciplinary action":**

**You're asking the wrong question.**

**Wrong question:** "Are they lying?"
**Right question:** "What process should I follow to fairly address this?"

**Fair management process:**

**1. Gather objective facts:**

**Ask directly:**
- "I saw social media posts from [concert/event] on Friday. Can you help me understand the timeline?"
- "Were the photos from a different date?"
- "Can you show me evidence these were old photos?" (if you're pursuing this - but be careful)

**Check:**
- Concert date vs. sick day date
- Photo timestamps if visible
- Who posted (their account vs. friend's account)

**2. Consider context:**

**Before assuming lying:**
- Is this first time? (Pattern vs. isolated incident)
- What's their track record? (Trustworthy employee vs. history of issues)
- Do you have other evidence? (Or just suspicion)
- How sick did they claim to be? ("Mild cold" vs. "food poisoning")

**3. Assess evidence standard:**

**For disciplinary action, you need:**
- Clear evidence of policy violation
- Not just suspicion
- Fair investigation
- Consistent application of policy

**"Suspicion based on social media" is weak basis for discipline without:**
- Proof photos were from sick day
- Proof employee posted them (not friend)
- Clear policy violation demonstrated

**4. Legal/HR considerations:**

**Before disciplinary action:**
- What does your sick leave policy actually say?
- Did they violate it? (Proof required)
- Have you applied same standard to others?
- Could this be discriminatory if wrong?
- What's your burden of proof?

**Consult HR if available.**

**What lying detection research shows:**

**People are terrible at detecting lies:**
- Accuracy around 50-54% (barely better than chance)
- "Gut feelings" about lying are unreliable
- Behavioral cues don't reliably indicate deception
- Even trained professionals struggle

**"Seems suspicious" is:**
- Your pattern-matching
- Potentially confirmation bias
- Not reliable evidence

**Possible approaches:**

**Option A: Direct conversation**

"I saw concert posts on Friday. I need to understand - were you well enough to attend, or were these from a different time? I want to give you chance to explain before making any decisions."

**Listen without prejudgment.**

**If explanation makes sense:** Accept it, note your concerns, move on
**If explanation doesn't make sense:** You can say so, but still need evidence for discipline

**Option B: Let it go**

**Unless:**
- Clear pattern of abuse
- Undeniable evidence (not just suspicion)
- Significant impact on team/work

**Consider:**
- One sick day, even if questionable, might not be worth investigation
- False accusations damage trust more than one sick day
- You might be wrong

**Option C: Document concerns**

- Note the situation
- Don't take action yet
- Watch for patterns
- If becomes pattern, address then

**What NOT to do:**

**Don't:**
- Assume guilt based on suspicion
- Investigate their social media obsessively
- Treat them as liar without proof
- Make disciplinary decision based on "seeming suspicious"
- Create hostile environment based on unproven suspicion
- Skip HR/legal consultation

**Do:**
- Follow fair process
- Gather facts
- Give them chance to explain
- Apply policies consistently
- Document everything
- Consult HR if serious

**Manager wisdom:**

**Sometimes employees do lie about sick days.**

**Also true:**
- Sometimes suspicious situations have innocent explanations
- Social media is deceptive about timing/context
- People get sick and recover quickly
- Friends post old photos constantly
- Your suspicion might be wrong

**Acting on suspicion without evidence:**
- Damages trust if you're wrong
- Creates legal risk
- Makes you look like bad manager
- Doesn't actually solve problem if they were lying (they'll just hide better next time)

**Better to:**
- Have direct conversation
- Establish clear expectations going forward
- Build culture where people don't feel need to lie
- Address patterns, not isolated incidents
