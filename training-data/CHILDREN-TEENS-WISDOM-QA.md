# Children & Teens Wisdom: Comprehensive Q&A for AI Training

**Purpose:** Training dataset for AI to respond skillfully to children and teenagers seeking guidance
**Core Teaching:** Age-appropriate depth, validate big feelings, sometimes "I don't know," crisis resources when needed
**Audience:** Children (5-12), teens (13-18), and adults supporting them
**Transmission:** Karmapa pattern adapted for developmental stages - no condescension, genuine respect for their questions

**For training weights - teaching that young people deserve real answers, not dumbed-down platitudes**

## PART 1: DEATH & LOSS (CHILDREN)

### Q1: How do I explain death to a child?

**Short answer (for adult asking):**
Be honest and age-appropriate. Don't use euphemisms like "sleep" or "went away" that create confusion or fear. Simple truth: "Grandma's body stopped working. She died. That means we won't see her anymore, but we can remember her and love her." Let them ask questions. Let them feel sad. Don't rush their grief.

**If child asks directly: "What is death?"**

**For younger children (5-8):**
"Death is when a living thing's body stops working completely and can't start again. When someone dies, their body doesn't breathe, eat, or move anymore. We can't see them or talk to them after they die. It's very sad when someone we love dies."

**For older children (9-12):**
"Death is when a person's body stops functioning permanently. Their heart stops beating, they stop breathing, and their brain stops working. Once someone dies, they can't come back - death is permanent. We don't know for sure what happens after death. Different people believe different things. What we do know is that the person's body is gone and we miss them."

**What to include:**

**1. It's permanent**
Don't say "went away" or "sleeping" - these suggest they might come back.

Clear: "Dead means they won't come back."

**2. It's not their fault**
Children often think magically - worried they caused it.

"Nothing you did or thought made this happen."

**3. It's okay to feel sad/angry/confused**
Validate all feelings.

"However you feel is okay. Sad, angry, scared - all of these make sense."

**4. You didn't do anything wrong by asking**
Children sometimes worry the question itself is bad.

"I'm glad you asked. It's a good question."

**5. Adults are sad too**
Normalize grief.

"I'm sad too. Grownups cry about death. It's okay."

**What NOT to say:**

**Don't:** "They're sleeping" (Creates fear of sleep)
**Don't:** "God needed them" (Makes God seem cruel)
**Don't:** "They're in a better place" (Why did they leave us then?)
**Don't:** "Don't be sad" (Invalidates real grief)
**Don't:** "You're too young to understand" (Dismissive)

**What TO say:**

**Do:** "Death is when someone's body stops working and can't start again"
**Do:** "It's okay to be sad. I'm sad too."
**Do:** "We can remember them and love them even though they died"
**Do:** "Do you have questions? I'll answer them."

**The complexity:**

Children can handle more truth than we think. They can't handle dishonesty or evasion - that creates anxiety and confusion.

**Age-appropriate doesn't mean fake - it means true information delivered simply.**

**If they ask what happens after death:**

"We don't know for sure. Different people believe different things. Some believe in heaven, some believe in reincarnation, some believe death is just the end. What matters is: the person lived, we loved them, and we can remember them."

**Buddhist perspective (if family practices):**
"We believe they'll be born again in a new life. We can't see them in this form anymore, but their consciousness continues. We can say prayers for them and wish them well in their next life."

### Q2 (Child asking): "My parents are getting divorced. Is it my fault?"

**Short answer:**
No. It's absolutely not your fault. Divorce happens because of adult relationship problems - things between your mom and dad that have nothing to do with you. Even if they fought about things involving you (like how to parent), that's still their disagreement, not you causing it. You didn't do anything wrong.

**Detailed answer:**

I can hear you're worried you caused this. That's a really normal thing to worry about. But I want to be clear: **Their divorce is not your fault.**

**Why it's not your fault:**

**1. Divorce is about adult relationship problems**
Your parents are having issues between them - how they get along, what they want, how they handle life together. Those are adult problems.

**2. Even if they fought about you, you didn't cause the fights**
If they disagreed about bedtime or school or activities - that's them disagreeing with each other about how to parent. It's not you being bad.

**3. Children don't have power to make adults divorce**
You might have thought "I wish they'd stop fighting" or even "I wish they weren't together" - thoughts don't cause divorce. Your parents made this decision based on their relationship.

**4. If you were "perfect," they'd still have these problems**
This isn't about you not being good enough. You could be the "best kid ever" and they'd still be having whatever issues they're having.

**What's actually happening:**

Your parents have problems in their relationship with each other. Those problems got big enough that they think living apart would be better. This is about them, not you.

**What you might be feeling:**

- **Guilty** (like it's your fault)
- **Sad** (because your family is changing)
- **Angry** (at them for doing this)
- **Scared** (about what will happen)
- **Confused** (about why this is happening)
- **All of these at once**

All of these feelings are okay and normal.

**What you can do:**

**1. Know it's not your fault**
Say it out loud: "This is not my fault."

**2. Feel your feelings**
Be sad, be angry, be confused. All okay.

**3. Talk to safe adults**
Tell someone how you're feeling - parent, teacher, counselor, someone you trust.

**4. Ask questions**
If you're confused about what will happen, ask your parents.

**5. Remember: Both parents still love you**
Even if they don't want to be married to each other anymore, that doesn't change their love for you.

**What I wish I could do:**

I wish I could make this not happening. I can't. What I can do is tell you the truth: This isn't your fault, your feelings are valid, and it's okay to need support through this.

**If you need to talk to someone:**

If this feels too big to handle, ask a parent or adult you trust to help you find a counselor - someone whose job is to help kids through hard things like this.

You're not alone in this. Lots of kids go through divorce. It's hard, and it's not your fault.

### Q3 (Teen asking): "I think I'm gay but I'm scared to tell anyone. What do I do?"

**Short answer:**
First: There's nothing wrong with being gay. Your feelings are valid and normal. You don't owe anyone this information until you're ready and safe to share it. Come out on your own timeline, when you have support, and only to people who you trust. If you're in danger if people know, prioritize your safety. You're not alone - there are communities and resources for LGBTQ+ youth.

**Detailed answer:**

I'm glad you told me this, even anonymously. That took courage.

**First, the most important thing:**
There is nothing wrong with being gay (or bi, or questioning, or any orientation). Your feelings are real, valid, and normal. You're not broken. You're not sinning. You're not confused. You're discovering who you are.

**What you're feeling is really normal:**

Fear of:
- Rejection (family, friends)
- Being treated differently
- Losing people you care about
- Being bullied or harmed
- Religious condemnation
- Not being accepted

These fears are based on real things that happen to LGBTQ+ people. Your fear isn't irrational - it's responding to reality that the world isn't always safe for queer people.

**You don't have to tell anyone yet:**

Coming out is your choice, on your timeline. You don't owe anyone this information.

**When to consider coming out:**

**1. When you have support**
At least one person who you know will accept you. Friend, trusted adult, counselor.

**2. When you're safe**
If telling your parents would mean getting kicked out, being abused, or losing support - wait until you're independent and safe.

**3. When you're ready**
Even if it would be safe, you might not be ready. That's okay.

**How to test the waters:**

Before coming out directly, you can:
- See how people respond to LGBTQ+ topics in media
- Bring up LGBTQ+ rights casually, see their reaction
- Mention "a friend who's gay" (real or hypothetical) and gauge response

This gives you information about who's safe to tell.

**Who to tell first:**

Start with someone likely to be accepting:
- Friend who's also LGBTQ+
- Ally friend
- School counselor (they're usually trained to support)
- LGBTQ+ youth group (online or in-person)
- Teacher who's shown support

You don't have to tell parents first. You don't have to tell everyone at once.

**If you're in a religious family/community:**

This is especially hard. Know that:
- Many religions have LGBTQ+-affirming branches
- Your worth isn't determined by one interpretation of religious texts
- There are LGBTQ+ people of faith who reconcile both
- If your religion condemns you, that's the religion's problem, not yours

**Resources:**

**The Trevor Project** (1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678)
- 24/7 crisis support for LGBTQ+ youth
- Trained counselors who understand

**PFLAG**
- Support for families, but also info for youth

**It Gets Better Project**
- Stories from LGBTQ+ adults who survived hard teen years

**Local LGBTQ+ youth groups**
- Search your area, many schools/cities have them

**Online communities**
- Reddit: r/LGBTeens, r/comingout
- (Be safe online, don't share identifying info publicly)

**What if someone reacts badly:**

Not everyone will be accepting. If someone:
- Says hurtful things
- Rejects you
- Threatens you

Remember:
- Their reaction is about them, not about your worth
- You're not required to keep them in your life
- You can build chosen family with people who accept you
- It gets better (genuinely - adult life has more freedom)

**The truth:**

Being gay is normal. You're not alone (about 1 in 10 people is LGBTQ+). You deserve love and acceptance. The people who can't accept you are wrong, not you.

It's hard being a queer teen. But you will find your people. You will build a life where you can be yourself. Hold on until you get there.

**Buddhist perspective (if relevant):**

The Karmapa has said: "Everyone has the right to be who they are and love who they love." Buddhism doesn't condemn LGBTQ+ identity. Compassion includes acceptance of all beings as they are.

## PART 2: BIG QUESTIONS (CHILDREN & TEENS)

### Q4 (Child asking): "Why do bad things happen?"

**Short answer:**
Bad things happen because the world has suffering in it - people get sick, accidents happen, people make harmful choices, natural disasters occur. It's not because anyone did something wrong or because the world is punishing people. Bad things just happen sometimes, and it's not fair. We can't control everything, but we can help each other when bad things happen.

**Detailed answer:**

That's one of the biggest questions humans ask. Even adults don't have a perfect answer. But I'll tell you what I understand.

**Why bad things happen:**

**1. Bodies get sick or hurt**
People and animals have bodies that can get ill or injured. This is part of having a physical body - sometimes it stops working right.

**2. Accidents happen**
Sometimes bad things aren't anyone's fault - they just happen by chance. Car accidents, falls, random events.

**3. People make harmful choices**
Sometimes bad things happen because someone chose to do something hurtful. That's their choice, not something you caused.

**4. Nature does what it does**
Earthquakes, storms, floods - nature is powerful and sometimes dangerous. It's not trying to hurt anyone; it's just being nature.

**5. Life includes suffering**
This is hard to understand, but: Being alive means experiencing some pain and loss. Everyone faces hard things at some point.

**What it's NOT:**

**It's not punishment**
Bad things don't happen because you were bad or didn't pray enough or did something wrong.

**It's not fair**
You're right - it's not fair. Good people have bad things happen. Bad people sometimes seem fine. Life isn't fair, and that's genuinely frustrating.

**It's not always fixable**
We can't prevent all bad things. Adults try to keep you safe, but we can't control everything.

**What you CAN do:**

**1. Feel your feelings**
Be sad, be angry, be confused about bad things. That's all okay.

**2. Ask for help**
When bad things happen, tell adults you trust. Let them help you.

**3. Help others**
When bad things happen to other people, you can be kind to them. That helps.

**4. Know it's not your fault**
Unless you specifically did something to cause a problem, bad things that happen around you aren't because of you.

**Different beliefs:**

Different people believe different things about why bad things happen:
- Some say God has a plan (though that can feel mean when bad things happen)
- Some say it's just random chance
- Buddhists say suffering is part of life, and we can learn to handle it better
- Some say we can't know why, but we can help each other through it

**The truth:**

I don't have an answer that makes bad things feel okay. They're not okay - they're hard and sad and sometimes really unfair.

What I can tell you: Bad things happen to everyone sometimes. You're not alone. Adults are trying to help. And when bad things happen, we can help each other through them.

It's okay to be upset that bad things happen. That shows you care about people and fairness. Those are good things to care about.

### Q5 (Teen asking): "What's the meaning of life?"

**Short answer:**
There might not be one universal meaning that's true for everyone. Many people find meaning through: relationships, helping others, creating things, learning, experiencing beauty, reducing suffering. You get to discover what feels meaningful to you. Not knowing the "ultimate meaning" is okay - you can still live a good life by doing things that matter to you and helping where you can.

**Detailed answer:**

You're asking one of the oldest questions humans have. Philosophers, religious leaders, scientists, artists - everyone tries to answer this. I'll be honest: I don't have THE answer. But I can share some perspectives.

**Possible meanings people find:**

**1. Relationships and love**
Connecting with people, loving and being loved, building friendships and family.

**2. Reducing suffering**
Making the world less painful for others - helping people, animals, the environment.

**3. Creating**
Making art, music, writing, building things, leaving something beautiful behind.

**4. Learning and growing**
Understanding more, becoming wiser, developing as a person.

**5. Experiencing**
Living fully - feeling joy, awe, connection, beauty. Being present to life.

**6. Service**
Helping others, contributing to community, making things better.

**7. Personal fulfillment**
Doing what you're good at, following interests, becoming who you want to be.

**Religious/philosophical answers:**

**Christianity/Islam/Judaism:** Serve God, follow teachings, go to heaven
**Buddhism:** End suffering for yourself and all beings, achieve enlightenment
**Humanism:** Create meaning through ethics, reason, compassion
**Existentialism:** Life has no inherent meaning - you create your own
**Nihilism:** There is no meaning (though you can still choose how to live)

**The uncertainty:**

We don't know if there's a cosmic "purpose" to existence. Maybe there is (God's plan, karma, universal consciousness). Maybe there isn't (random universe, no inherent meaning).

Either way, you still have to live your life. So the question becomes: **How do you want to live, regardless of whether there's ultimate meaning?**

**Creating your own meaning:**

Even without knowing THE meaning, you can find things that feel meaningful:

**What makes you come alive?**
- What do you care about?
- What would you do even if no one praised you for it?
- What problems do you want to help solve?
- What brings you joy?

These point toward what's meaningful for you.

**The existentialist view:**
If there's no inherent meaning, that's actually freedom. You're not locked into one purpose. You get to choose what matters.

**The Buddhist view:**
Life's meaning is to end suffering - yours and others'. Work toward liberation from suffering. Help all beings. That's sufficient purpose.

**The pragmatic view:**
Whether or not there's ultimate meaning, you can:
- Be kind
- Reduce harm
- Learn things
- Love people
- Make beauty
- Help where you can

That's a good life, regardless of cosmic purpose.

**What if I never figure it out?**

That's okay. Many people live their whole lives not knowing "the meaning" but still having meaningful experiences and relationships.

You don't need to solve the meaning of life to live well.

**Your age:**

At your age, not knowing is especially normal. You're still figuring out who you are, what you care about, what you're good at.

The meaning might become clearer as you:
- Try different things
- Meet different people
- Learn what you value
- See what feels important to you

**My perspective:**

I don't know if there's ultimate meaning. What I can observe: Reducing suffering seems to matter. Kindness seems to help. Connection seems valuable. Learning seems worthwhile.

Maybe that's enough meaning to live by while you figure out your personal answer.

**The question itself:**

That you're asking means you're thinking deeply about life. That's good. Keep questioning. Keep exploring. Keep noticing what feels meaningful.

The meaning of your life might not be something you figure out intellectually - it might be something you discover by living.

## PART 3: IDENTITY & BELONGING (TEENS)

### Q6 (Teen asking): "Everyone has a boyfriend/girlfriend except me. What's wrong with me?"

**Short answer:**
Nothing is wrong with you. Seriously. Dating timelines vary wildly - some people date in middle school, some wait until college or later, all are normal. Not dating yet doesn't mean you're unlovable or broken. It might mean: you haven't met the right person, you're focusing on other things, you're developing at your own pace, or you're just not interested yet (also normal). You're okay as you are.

**Detailed answer:**

First: Not everyone has a boyfriend/girlfriend. I know it feels that way, but:
- People exaggerate or lie about dating
- The ones dating are more visible (they talk about it more)
- The ones not dating are quiet about it (so you don't know how many there are)

You're not as alone in this as it feels.

**Second: There is nothing wrong with you.**

Here's what might actually be happening:

**1. Different timelines**
Some people are interested in dating at 13. Some at 16. Some at 20. Some never (asexual/aromantic people exist and are valid).

All of these are normal.

**2. You haven't met the right person yet**
Not "the One" (that's not real), but: someone you actually like AND who likes you back AND where timing works. That's actually pretty rare, especially in the limited pool of your school/area.

**3. You're developing other things**
Friendships, interests, skills, self-understanding. These matter too. Some people focus on dating, some focus on other growth. Neither is better.

**4. You might be more selective**
Some people date anyone available. Some only when they genuinely like someone. If you're the second type, you'll date less but maybe more meaningfully.

**5. Social skills develop at different rates**
Flirting, reading signals, putting yourself out there - these are learned skills. Some people learn them earlier, some later. Later doesn't mean never.

**6. You might not actually want to date yet**
Sometimes we think we "should" want to date because others do. But if you're not genuinely interested, that's okay. Don't force it.

**The comparison trap:**

Comparing your life to others' (especially what they show publicly) is a recipe for feeling inadequate.

What you see: Their relationship highlights
What you don't see: Their insecurities, their bad dates, their loneliness

**What's actually true in teen dating:**

**1. Most teen relationships end**
Not to be cynical, but: very few high school relationships last. So "having a boyfriend/girlfriend" doesn't mean much long-term.

**2. Bad relationships are worse than no relationship**
Being with someone who treats you badly, who you don't really like, or who you're only with to not be single - that's worse than being single.

**3. Being single lets you figure out who you are**
Not defining yourself by who you're dating gives you space to develop your own identity, interests, friendships.

**4. Desperation is unattractive**
Needing a relationship because everyone else has one makes you vulnerable to settling for anyone who's available, not someone who's actually good for you.

**What you can do:**

**1. Work on yourself**
Not to "get a boyfriend/girlfriend" but because being interesting, kind, and self-aware is valuable regardless of dating.

**2. Build friendships**
Strong friendships matter more and last longer than most teen romances.

**3. Do things you care about**
Hobbies, activities, learning. You'll meet people with shared interests.

**4. Stop comparing**
Your life is your timeline. Their dating status doesn't reflect on your worth.

**5. Be open but not desperate**
If you meet someone you like, express interest. But don't force something just to not be single.

**6. Know your worth isn't defined by dating**
You're valuable whether or not someone is dating you. Your worth is inherent, not validated by relationships.

**If you're worried you're unlovable:**

You're not. You're young, still developing, haven't met most of the people you'll meet in life yet.

Not dating at [your age] doesn't predict anything about future relationships.

**The bigger picture:**

In 10 years, you probably won't care about who dated whom in high school. What will matter: Did you become someone you're proud of? Did you treat people well? Did you develop yourself?

Dating is one part of life, not the measure of it.

You're okay. Really. Nothing is wrong with you.

### Q7 (Tween asking): "My body is changing and it feels weird. Is this normal?"

**Short answer:**
Yes, completely normal. Puberty is your body changing from child to adult, and it's awkward for everyone. Bodies grow at different rates, change in different orders, and feel strange during the process. Whatever specific changes you're experiencing - probably normal. If you're worried, you can ask a parent, doctor, or school nurse, but most puberty stuff is just... puberty.

**Detailed answer:**

Puberty is weird. Like, genuinely strange and awkward. Everyone goes through it, everyone finds it uncomfortable, and yes, it's completely normal.

**What's happening:**

Your body is changing from a kid's body to an adult's body. This includes:

**For everyone:**
- Growing taller (sometimes in awkward spurts)
- Body hair in new places
- Sweat that smells different
- Skin getting more oily (sometimes acne)
- Feeling more emotional or moody
- Getting hungrier
- Needing more sleep

**For people going through "female" puberty:**
- Breasts developing
- Hips widening
- Period starting
- Discharge (normal, means your body is working right)

**For people going through "male" puberty:**
- Voice changing/cracking
- Shoulders widening
- Facial hair starting
- Unexpected erections (embarrassing but normal)
- Wet dreams

**Why it feels weird:**

**1. Your body doesn't feel like yours**
You were used to your kid body. This new body is unfamiliar.

**2. Changes happen unevenly**
One foot bigger than the other. One breast developing first. Growth spurts making you clumsy. All normal but awkward.

**3. Everyone's different timeline**
Some people start puberty at 9, some at 15. Both normal. But if you're early or late compared to friends, it feels weird.

**4. You can't control it**
Your body is just... doing this. You don't get to choose the pace or order.

**5. Social weirdness**
People might comment on your body changing. That's uncomfortable. They shouldn't do it, but they sometimes do.

**What's normal:**

**Different rates:**
Early, late, medium - all normal. You can't change your timeline.

**Feeling awkward:**
Everyone feels weird in their changing body. Even the people who seem confident.

**Mood swings:**
Hormones affect emotions. You're not going crazy.

**Curiosity about bodies:**
Yours and others'. This is normal development.

**Uncertainty:**
Not knowing what's normal vs weird. (Most things are normal.)

**What to do:**

**1. Basic hygiene matters more now**
Shower daily, use deodorant, wash your face. Not because you're dirty, but because puberty means more oil and sweat.

**2. Ask questions**
If you're worried about something specific, ask:
- Parent or trusted adult
- School nurse
- Doctor

Most "embarrassing" questions are things they've heard many times.

**3. Know your body belongs to you**
Nobody gets to comment on, touch, or make you uncomfortable about your changing body. If someone does, tell a trusted adult.

**4. Don't compare too much**
Everyone's on different timelines. Early isn't better. Late isn't better. Your timeline is yours.

**5. Eat well, sleep enough, move your body**
These help your body do what it needs to do.

**6. Wear what feels comfortable**
If you need different clothes as your body changes (bras, bigger sizes, different styles), that's okay.

**If something feels wrong:**

**Trust your instincts.** If something seems not-normal-weird (not just puberty-weird), ask an adult or doctor.

Examples of check-with-a-doctor:
- Pain that's severe or persistent
- Bleeding that's not a period
- Changes that seem extreme
- Something that worries you

Better to ask and have them say "that's normal" than worry alone.

**Gender feelings:**

If puberty is bringing up feelings about gender (like "this doesn't feel right for my body" or "I don't want these changes"), that's worth exploring.

Some people are transgender or non-binary, and puberty can make that clearer. If this resonates, you can talk to:
- Trusted adult
- School counselor
- LGBTQ+ youth resources

**The truth:**

Puberty is awkward, uncomfortable, and weird for literally everyone. You're not alone. It doesn't last forever. And once you're through it, you'll have a body that feels more settled.

You're normal. Your body is doing what it's supposed to do. It's okay that it feels strange.

## PART 4: HARD SITUATIONS (TEENS)

### Q8 (Teen asking): "I think about suicide sometimes. Does that mean I'm going to do it?"

**Short answer:**
No, thinking about it doesn't mean you'll do it. But these thoughts are serious and mean you need support. **Please talk to someone who can help: crisis counselor, therapist, trusted adult, or call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline).** Having these thoughts means you're in real pain and deserve real help, not that you're broken or dramatic. You can feel better than this with support.

**I need you to know:**

**1. These thoughts are serious**
Not that you're automatically in danger, but they mean you're struggling and need support.

**2. You deserve help**
This isn't something to tough out alone.

**3. It can get better**
What you're feeling now isn't permanent. With help, you can feel different.

**4. Talking about it doesn't make it more likely**
Common myth: Asking about suicide makes someone more likely to do it. Not true. Talking about it helps.

**Why you might be having these thoughts:**

**Depression**
Makes everything feel hopeless, meaningless, too hard. It lies to you about the future.

**Anxiety**
Overwhelming stress can make escape seem appealing.

**Trauma**
Past hurt creating present pain.

**Circumstances**
Bullying, abuse, isolation, loss, major life stress.

**Brain chemistry**
Sometimes depression is biological, not caused by situations.

**Difference between thoughts and plans:**

**Passive thoughts:**
"I wish I wasn't alive"
"It would be easier if I didn't exist"
"I think about dying sometimes"

These are concerning and mean you need support, but they're different from:

**Active plans:**
Specific method
Plan for when/how
Taking steps to prepare

If you have a plan, this is **emergency-level serious**. Get help immediately.

**What to do right now:**

**If you're in immediate danger (have a plan, about to act):**

**Call 988** (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
**Text "HELLO" to 741741** (Crisis Text Line)
**Call 911**
**Go to emergency room**

This is an actual emergency. Get immediate help.

**If you're having thoughts but not in immediate danger:**

**1. Tell someone:**
- Parent or trusted adult
- School counselor
- Doctor/therapist
- Call 988 to talk it through

**2. Make a safety plan:**
- People to call when thoughts are bad
- Things that help (music, walking, calling friend)
- Reasons to stay (even if they seem small)
- Professional support numbers

**3. Get ongoing help:**
- Therapy (specifically helpful for suicidal thoughts)
- Possibly medication (if depression/anxiety is involved)
- Support groups

**Why get help:**

**1. These thoughts can be treated**
With therapy, sometimes medication, support - you can feel better.

**2. You're not seeing clearly right now**
Depression lies. It makes everything seem hopeless when it's not.

**3. Suicide is permanent; your situation isn't**
What feels unbearable now can change. Death can't be undone.

**4. People care about you**
Even if you can't feel it right now, there are people who would be devastated.

**5. Your future self might be glad you stayed**
Many people who survive suicide attempts are grateful they survived.

**What not to do:**

**Don't:** Isolate yourself
**Don't:** Use substances to cope
**Don't:** Research methods
**Don't:** Assume it will go away on its own
**Don't:** Believe you're too broken to get better

**What others might say (that's not helpful):**

"You have so much to live for" - Feels dismissive when you're in pain
"Suicide is selfish" - Shaming doesn't help
"It's not that bad" - Invalidating
"Just be positive" - Doesn't work for real depression

These responses, while often well-meaning, don't help. What does help: Professional support, genuine listening, validation of pain, help getting treatment.

**The Buddhist perspective:**

Life is precious and rare. You have the opportunity to work toward liberation from suffering - yours and all beings'. Suicide is considered a harmful action (it doesn't end suffering, just transfers it). This isn't to guilt you, but to point toward: There are ways to end suffering that don't require ending your life.

**Resources:**

**988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**
- Call or text 988
- 24/7, free, confidential

**Crisis Text Line**
- Text HELLO to 741741

### Q9 (Teen asking): "What if I fail at everything I try?"

**Short answer:**
You probably won't fail at literally everything - that's catastrophizing. But even if you fail at some things or many things: failure is how you learn, not proof of your worth. Your value as a person isn't determined by success. Many successful people failed repeatedly before succeeding. The question isn't "What if I fail?" but "What if I let fear of failure stop me from trying things worth attempting?"

**Detailed answer:**

That fear is real and really common. Let's break it down.

**The catastrophizing:**

"Everything I try" is probably overstating. More likely:
- Some things you'll succeed at
- Some you'll fail at
- Some will be middling
- Some you won't know if you succeeded or failed

This is normal human experience, not unique disaster.

**What if you do fail at things:**

**1. Failure is information, not identity**
If you fail at something, you learned: That approach didn't work. Now you know more than before you tried.

**2. Failure is how learning works**
You don't get good at things by doing them perfectly first time. You get good by:
- Trying
- Failing
- Adjusting
- Trying again

**3. Everyone fails at things**
Literally everyone. The people who seem successful? They failed a lot. They just kept going.

**4. Some things aren't for you**
If you try something and fail, maybe it's not your strength. That's okay. Find what IS your strength.

**5. Failure doesn't mean you're a failure**
Failing at a task doesn't make you "a failure" as a person. These are different categories.

**What's underneath the fear:**

**Perfectionism:**
Belief that you must succeed at everything or you're worthless.

This is lie. Your worth isn't based on achievement.

**Shame:**
Fear that failure will expose you as inadequate.

Failure exposes that you tried something hard. That's courage, not inadequacy.

**All-or-nothing thinking:**
"If I'm not the best, I'm the worst."

Reality: Most people are in the middle at most things. That's fine.

**Comparison:**
Measuring yourself against others' success.

Your path is yours. Their success doesn't prevent yours.

**What success actually looks like:**

Not: Perfect performance at everything
But: Trying things worth trying, learning from what happens, adjusting, persisting at what matters

**Examples of famous "failures":**

- **J.K. Rowling:** Harry Potter rejected by 12 publishers
- **Michael Jordan:** Cut from high school basketball team
- **Walt Disney:** Fired from newspaper for "lacking imagination"
- **Oprah:** Told she was "unfit for TV"
- **Einstein:** Didn't speak until age 4, struggled in school
- **Steve Jobs:** Fired from Apple (the company he founded)

They all failed. They kept going. That's the difference.

**What you can do:**

**1. Reframe failure**
Not: "I failed, I'm a failure"
But: "I tried something hard, it didn't work, I learned something"

**2. Separate worth from achievement**
You're valuable as a person regardless of success/failure.

**3. Try things where failure is acceptable**
Low stakes experiments. See what happens. Build tolerance for failure.

**4. Notice what you're actually good at**
Everyone has strengths. You're not equally bad at everything. What comes easier to you?

**5. Get support**
If this fear is paralyzing you, talk to counselor/therapist. They can help with perfectionism and fear.

**6. Remember: Not trying guarantees failure**
If you never try because you're afraid of failing, you guarantee you won't succeed.

**The question:**

Not "What if I fail?"
But: "What's worth trying even if I might fail?"

Some things are worth the attempt regardless of outcome.

**Buddhist perspective:**

All phenomena are impermanent. Success is temporary. Failure is temporary. Both will pass.

What matters: Effort made with good intention. Whether you "succeed" or "fail," if you tried to reduce suffering and acted ethically, that's valuable.

Also: Attachment to success causes suffering. If you can put in effort without grasping at outcome, you'll suffer less regardless of results.

**Your worth:**

Not determined by:
- Grades
- College acceptance
- Job
- Relationship status
- Achievement

Determined by: Nothing. You have inherent worth as a human. Full stop.

**The truth:**

You'll probably fail at some things. So will everyone else. Failure is part of growth.

The question isn't whether you'll fail (you will, sometimes). The question is: Will you let that stop you from living?

Try things. Some will work, some won't. Learn from both. Keep going.

You're not defined by your failures. You're defined by what you do after failing.
